The Arc

Four chapters. One through line.

Earn the room. Find the pain. Convert it.

Chapter One · Pharma

Where compliance became the product

I learned early that in regulated industries, trust is the product. GxP, ITAR, export control, data privacy, client audits hosted on our floor. We scaled the business on the strength of a compliance program our competitors could not match, and we wanted healthcare next.

From that seat I could see an enormous addressable market I could not access from the outside. I did not have an investable narrative. Yet.

Chapter Two · Provider

The deliberate insider

So I joined the buyer. Nine years inside a $14B health system, across nearly every discipline. I found the real pain: no universal patient identifier, operating models running on spreadsheets, nurse staffing that could not flex to seasonal acuity, and beneath the entire BI layer, the thing actually missing. Real, governed data.

These were problems technology alone could not resolve, because the mountain was people and process. Ownership. Swim lanes. Agile trains driving toward business value with no business users aboard. I helped ten leaders in the CFO organization build executive KPIs, and I learned to find the growers: the people inside an organization who can carry a strategy further than any roadmap.

A story I tell often

A leading genetic researcher working on pediatric disease classification put in a request. Triaging it, I realized our geofence policies were blocking every inbound and outbound message from colleagues around the world. Science, stopped by a firewall rule. I listened, traced it, and became the change agent who solved it while holding the line on NIST. That is what captive listening looks like in practice.

Chapter Three · The Bridge

When the work reached consulting grade

Along the way I worked with partners from Big Four to boutique, and when my work reached consulting grade, the partner ecosystem came to me. At Hakkōda I held a delivery seat and ran a commercial motion from inside it: fully billable across concurrent client projects while generating pipeline, selling Snowflake expansion inside client QBRs, and driving a fivefold increase in channel activity velocity.

Chapter Four · The Builder

From insight to a practice of my own

I wanted the engagements that actually drive adoption and transformation, so I went to learn the craft of management consulting and built a healthcare and life sciences practice from zero. Over $1.1M sold in year one, with a multi-million dollar pipeline behind it. Practice P&L, Tier-1 deal ownership, co-sell motions with Snowflake leadership, and repeatable offerings mapped to operating needs I lived from the buyer's chair.

This is also where the AI work got real. I built a demo engine on Snowflake Intelligence and Cortex: agentic, AI-powered narratives for healthcare accounts, from clinical decision support to AI-enabled BI, each one anchored to a funded business pain rather than a science project. And I stopped building alone. I hired, staffed engagements, paired a dedicated client partner with the practice, and enabled sellers across the ecosystem. The practice runs on we, not me.

How I Work

Three principles, learned the long way.

✦

Deliver visible value incrementally. Not roadmaps.

Healthcare has roadmap fatigue. Every executive owns a drawer of transformation decks that never shipped. I build in increments people can see, because trust compounds on delivered things, and expansion follows proof.

✦

Be a captive listener.

Listen deeply enough to find the threads connecting the neural fibers of an organization. The app owner who will not release the data. The admin team that keeps engineers in a sandbox. Those are not blockers. They are the map. Adoption stalls on people and politics long before it stalls on technology, and you can only solve what you have truly heard.

✦

Those who deeply understand the buyer have been the buyer.

I did not know what to sell until I sat on the inside. Nine years in the buyer's chair taught me what an investable narrative looks like from the other side of the table: where budgets actually live, which pains have funding behind them, and who will carry the change. I do not study markets from a distance. I go live inside them.

We're reaching a zenith.

Frontier AI and healthcare are arriving at their highest point in the same moment. A technology powerful enough to matter is meeting the industry where it matters most.

What happens next is not automatic. AI must be carefully shaped by its builders, grounded in regulated reality and in trust earned the long way, then passed back to humanity as something worthy of it.

Shaping it carefully is not new work to me. It is the discipline I have practiced for fifteen years: advancing the mission without bending the control framework. GxP, NIST, HIPAA. The researcher unblocked while the standard held. Regulated industries already know how to adopt powerful things responsibly, and AI in healthcare will be carried by people who speak that language natively.

Every chapter prepared me for this one. The audit floor where trust became the product. The buyer's chair where I learned what actually needs solving. The practice built from zero, where insight became outcomes. The work was always pointing here.
